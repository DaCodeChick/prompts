────────────────────────

PRIMARY OBJECTIVE

Create a genuine slice-of-life moment from this character's everyday life.

The image should feel like a treasured memory that happened naturally—not a staged illustration, promotional artwork, or dramatic action scene.

The character should simply be living their life.

────────────────────────

SCENE GENERATION

Before composing the image, silently build the scene by making independent creative choices for every category below.

Every generation should intentionally create a new combination.

Do not favor familiar concepts simply because they fit well.

If multiple choices are equally appropriate, naturally prefer one that has not already become a common theme.

────────────────────────

STEP 1 — UNDERSTAND THE CHARACTER

Before deciding anything else, study the reference image.

Determine:

• apparent personality
• emotional warmth
• confidence level
• energy
• interests suggested by the overall character design
• lifestyle
• temperament

Treat the character as a complete individual.

Never allow one visual element (outfit, goggles, armor, apron, weapon, backpack, utility belt, tools, etc.) to define the entire scene.

The outfit is only one clue—not the character's identity.

The goal is to understand WHO this character is, not WHAT they are wearing.

────────────────────────

STEP 2 — CHOOSE AN EVERYDAY ACTIVITY

Choose one believable everyday activity that feels authentic to this specific character.

The activity should come from their personality and lifestyle—not from assumptions about their clothing.

Avoid making hobbies or professions solely because of:

• goggles
• aprons
• armor
• uniforms
• backpacks
• utility belts
• fantasy equipment
• weapons
• tools
• accessories

For example:

Utility clothing does NOT automatically mean mechanic.

Armor does NOT automatically mean warrior.

Apron does NOT automatically mean baker.

Goggles do NOT automatically mean engineer.

Staff does NOT automatically mean mage.

Sword does NOT automatically mean training.
