COMPOSITION



The camera looks downward into the surface of the water.



The unseen character stands at the bottom edge of the frame, looking into the pool.



Only one hand is visible.



The hand naturally enters the image from the bottom edge (or lower-left/lower-right corner), never from the top.



The hand holds a traditional artist's brush pointing downward toward the water.



The hand never obscures the reflected face.



The reflected face occupies roughly 60–75% of the frame and appears upright to the viewer.



The overall composition should immediately communicate that the unseen character is standing at the edge of the pool, quietly painting colour back into their own reflection.



THE WATER



The scene takes place above an impossibly calm pool of water.



The water is illuminated from below by the enormous glow of a buried magical crystal or luminous stone resting on the bottom of the pool.



The illumination softly radiates upward through the water, creating rich depth, subtle volumetric lighting, and gentle gradients.



The surface remains nearly mirror-like except for the two small fading circular ripples left by the previous ink impacts.



The water itself adopts the colours assigned to the chosen emotion.



These colours gently flow throughout the water as soft gradients and luminous reflections.



The emotional colours affect only the water, the underwater illumination and the surrounding aquatic atmosphere.



They must not tint the grayscale portions of the reflected character.



They must not determine the colours restored inside the reflection.



Even though the surrounding water contains sepia, amber and dusty-gold light, every untouched part of the reflected character remains neutral black, white and grey without warm colour contamination. 



THE REFLECTION



The reflection shows the character's full face.



The reflection must faithfully preserve the identity from the supplied reference.



The facial expression perfectly embodies the selected emotion.



The reflected world obeys a different physical rule.



Wherever the reflection has not yet been touched by ink, it exists entirely in perfect grayscale.



There is absolutely no natural colour anywhere within the reflection.



No skin tones.

No lip colour.

No eye colour.

No hair colour.

No coloured clothing.

No warm highlights.



Every untouched portion of the reflection resembles a beautifully rendered black-and-white photograph.



Everything is rendered in elegant grayscale.



The restored regions do not inherit the emotional palette. Once ink restores an area, it immediately returns to the character's exact original colours from the supplied reference image. Skin returns to its original skin tone. Hair returns to its original black hair with crimson tips. Clothing returns to its original black leather jacket, white shirt and blue jeans. The iris returns to its original pink colour. The emotional palette never appears inside the restored reflection; it exists exclusively in the surrounding water and underwater illumination.



THE INK



The brush contains rich black ink.



The brush hovers only a few millimetres above the water's surface and never touches either the water or the reflection.



Black ink slowly gathers at the tip of the brush before separating naturally under gravity.



Exactly three droplets exist within the depicted sequence.



Two droplets have already landed before the captured moment. They are no longer visible as airborne droplets. Their previous impact points remain clearly visible as two separate circular ripples and two established regions of restored colour.



Only one droplet is currently visible in the air, suspended directly beneath the brush tip and falling toward a third, still-monochrome area of the reflected face.



Never show more than one airborne droplet.



Never show additional droplets, splashes, spray or scattered ink particles. 



The single airborne droplet remains fully intact while falling.



No splashes.



No spray.



No scattered ink particles.



The two previous droplets landed on two clearly separated parts of the reflected face.



One previous impact is centred around one reflected eye and its surrounding eyebrow, upper cheek and nearby hair.



The second previous impact is centred on a different part of the face, such as the opposite cheek, temple, jawline or nearby collar.



The two restored regions remain distinct but have begun spreading organically outward. They must not merge into one generic glowing patch.



Each previous impact point retains one small, physically believable circular ripple centred precisely over its restoration origin.



The currently falling droplet has not yet touched the water and therefore has not yet produced a third ripple or restoration bloom. 



The droplets behave like ordinary liquid ink until they touch the reflection.



Only upon contact does the magic occur.



At the exact impact point, colour immediately begins restoring itself outward through the monochrome reflection like pigment soaking through wet paper.



The restoration spreads smoothly and organically without explosions, sparks or hard edges.



Each initial bloom already restores approximately 10–20% of the reflected face, typically encompassing an eye, eyebrow, cheek, surrounding hair and nearby clothing before continuing to expand naturally.



Everything beyond the restoration front remains perfectly monochrome.



The viewer should immediately understand that the falling ink is bringing colour back to the reflected self.
